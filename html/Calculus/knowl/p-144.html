The situation is more complicated when \(x \to -2\text{,}\) because \(f(-2)\) is not defined. If we try to use a similar algebraic argument regarding the numerator and denominator, we observe that as \(x \to -2\text{,}\) \((4-x^2) \to (4 - (-2)^2) = 0\text{,}\) and \((x+2) \to (-2 + 2) = 0\text{,}\) so as \(x \to -2\text{,}\) the numerator and denominator of \(f\) both tend to 0. We call \(0/0\) an indeterminate form. This tells us that there is somehow more work to do. From Table 1.14 and Figure 1.15, it appears that \(f\) should have a limit of \(4\) at \(x = -2\text{.}\)
