  1. Since \(S_1 = 1\) and \(T_1 = \frac{1}{2}\text{,}\) we see that \(S_1 \gt T_1\text{.}\)

  2. Since \(a_2 = \frac{1}{4}\) and \(b_2 = \frac{1}{6}\text{,}\) it is the case that \(a_2 \gt b_2\text{.}\) We already know that \(S_1 \gt T_1\text{,}\) so adding a larger number to \(S_1\) than we add to \(T_1\) makes \(S_2 \gt T_2\text{.}\)

  3. Since \(a_3 = \frac{1}{9}\) and \(b_3 = \frac{1}{12}\text{,}\) it is the case that \(a_3 \gt b_3\text{.}\) We already know that \(S_2 \gt T_2\text{,}\) so adding a larger number to \(S_2\) than we add to \(T_2\) makes \(S_3 \gt T_3\text{.}\)

Since \(n^2+n \gt n^2\) when \(n\) is positive, we have \(\frac{1}{n^2} \gt \frac{1}{n^2+n}\) or \(a_n \gt b_n\text{.}\) So at each step, we are adding larger numbers to the partial sums of \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) than we are to the partial sums of \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\text{,}\) we conclude that \(S_n \gt T_n\text{.}\)

  5. Since \(S_n \gt T_n\text{,}\) we should expect \(\lim_{n \to \infty} S_n \gt \lim_{n \to \infty} T_n\) or

    \begin{equation*} \sum \frac{1}{k^2} \gt \sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\text{.} \end{equation*}

    Since \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) is a \(p\)-series with \(p=2 \gt 1\text{,}\) we know that \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2}\) converges (and is finite). Now \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\) is a sum of positive terms and is therefore positive, and is also less than a finite number (\(\sum \frac{1}{k^2}\)), so we expect that \(\sum \frac{1}{k^2+k}\) converges as well.
