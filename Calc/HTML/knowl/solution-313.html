Solution8.1.8.1

  1. Sampling the function at the endpoints of \(8\) equal subintervals of length \(10/8 = 1.25\text{,}\) we see the data pictured in the following figure (with the sample points in red). While the function is periodic with a period of \(\frac{2\pi}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) the \(9\) data points do not appear periodic at all as none of them has repeated (yet) over the course of the pictured interval.

  2. Since the period of the function \(f(x) = \sin(4x)\) is \(P = \frac{\pi}{2}\text{,}\) its frequency is \(\frac{2}{\pi} \approx 0.6366\text{,}\) which means that this function completes about \(0.6366\) cycles per second. Doubling this frequncy gives us a value of \(\frac{4}{\pi} \approx 1.2732\text{.}\) This means that we should sample the function at at least a rate of twice per cycle, or at least \(1.2732\) samples per second. On a 10-second interval, we therefore need at least \(13\) samples, so we should do so at least every \(10/13 \approx 0.76923\) seconds.

  3. Since the theorem says to sample at a minimum of double the maximum desired frequency, and humans can't detect signals above \(20\) KHz, it follows that double \(20\) KHz is \(40\) KHz, or \(40000\) Hz, so sampling at \(44100\) Hz slightly exceeds the minimum sampling needed by the formal result.
